The Old Man and The Sea

The Old Man and the Sea is a novel by Ernest Hemingway, published in 1952. It follows Santiago, an aging Cuban fisherman who has gone 84 days without catching a fish. Determined to end his unlucky streak, he ventures far into the Gulf Stream, where he hooks a massive marlin. After an intense three-day struggle, he finally kills the fish, only for sharks to attack and eat most of it on his journey home. In the end, Santiago returns to shore with only the fish’s skeleton, exhausted but undefeated in spirit. The novel explores themes of perseverance, resilience, and man's struggle against nature.

Main Characters

  • Santiago: An old, experienced fisherman who embodies strength, determination, and wisdom. He refuses to give up despite his misfortunes. The character who we accompnay througout the book, and will deeply sympathize with.
  • Manolin: A young boy who deeply admires and cares for Santiago. Though his parents prevent him from fishing with the old man due to Santiago’s bad luck, he remains devoted to him. He will be present only in the thoughts of Santiago for most of the story.
  • The Marlin: A massive, noble fish that Santiago battles for days. It symbolizes both the beauty and the cruelty of nature. It will serve as Santiago's spiritual companion for the days where he will sail back to Cuban soil.
  • The Sharks: Predatory creatures that devour Santiago’s marlin, representing destruction and the inevitable forces that challenge human success. They are the antagonists of the story, but not the narrative.

Themes

  • Perseverance: Santiago’s determination to catch the marlin despite his age and bad luck reflects the novel’s theme of perseverance. He refuses to give up, even when faced with overwhelming odds.
  • Resilience: Santiago’s ability to endure physical and emotional hardships demonstrates the theme of resilience. He remains strong and hopeful, even in the face of defeat.

Review

The novel is a force of nature in itself, Hemingway's writing leaves a deep marks in it's reader, and implores in us a deep respect for nature. The forces in the story are wonderfully balanced, the Old Man Santiago, who is poor but richer than most in his understanding of nature's bounties and cruelties. The Marlin, who is almost otherworldy in beauty, is both Santiago's deliverance from his plight, and nature's uncaring cycle of life and death. The Marlin itself is the sea, and at the same time, Santiago himself is also the sea. A novel that everyone should read, it is a classic through and through.
